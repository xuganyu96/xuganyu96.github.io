## Schoolbook multiplication

Let $$a, b$$ each be a base-$$D$$ number with up to $$n$$ digits:

$$
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    a &= a_0 + a_1 \cdot D + \ldots + a_{n-1} \cdot D^{n-1} \\
    b &= b_0 + b_1 \cdot D + \ldots + b_{n-1} \cdot D^{n-1} \\
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

We are interested in computing their product, which we will denote by $$z$$.
It's easy to see that $$z$$ has up to $$2n$$ digits. With the naive schoolbook
multiplication, every digit of $$a$$ needs to multiply with every digit of
$$b$$, resulting in a total of $$n^2$$ digit multiplications. In asymptotic
notation, we say that *schoolbook multiplication has $$O(n^2)$$ complexity*.
Note that we ignored the cost of addition in this estimation. While
asymptotically this analysis is correct since with sufficiently large $$n$$,
the cost of addition is negligible compared to the cost of multiplication, in
practice (e.g. with the parameters used in HQC), the cost of addition is smaller
but not negligible. Consequently, using Karatsuba, which trades the number of
multiplications for the number of additions, only makes sense for sufficiently
large $$n$$.

## Karatsuba

Divide $$a, b$$ into higher and lower order halves. For example, the
higher-order half will have up to $$n_\text{high}$$ digits and the lower-order
half has up to $$n_\text{low}$$ digits ($$n = n_\text{high} + n_\text{low}$$):

$$
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    a &= a_\text{high} \cdot D^{n_\text{low}} + a_\text{low} \\
    b &= b_\text{high} \cdot D^{n_\text{low}} + b_\text{low} \\
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

It is straightforward to arithmetically verify the truth of the following
equation:

$$
\begin{equation}\label{eq:divide-and-conquer}
\begin{aligned}
& (a_\text{high} \cdot D^{n_\text{low}} + a_\text{low})
\cdot
(b_\text{high} \cdot D^{n_\text{low}} + b_\text{low}) \\
&= a_\text{high}b_\text{high}D^{2n_\text{low}} \\
    &+ (
        (a_\text{high} + a_\text{low})(b_\text{high} + b_\text{low})
        - a_\text{high}b_\text{high} - a_\text{low}b_\text{low}
    )D^{n_\text{low}} \\
    &+ a_\text{low} b_\text{low}
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

Notice on the RHS of Equation $$\ref{eq:divide-and-conquer}$$, there are three
unique products:

- The "high" product: $$a_\text{high}b_\text{high}$$
- The "low" product: $$a_\text{low}b_\text{low}$$
- The "mid" product: $$(a_\text{high} + a_\text{low})(b_\text{high} + b_\text{low})$$

In programming we can ignore the cost of multiplying by $$D$$ by choosing $$D$$
to be a power of $$2$$ and thus converting the complex multiplication into
simple bit shifting.

We can choose $$n_\text{high}, n_\text{low}$$ to be roughly equal and thus each
roughly equal to half of $$n$$. Equation $$\ref{eq:divide-and-conquer}$$ shows
that the multiplication of two $$n$$-digit numbers can be accomplished using
three multiplications of $$(n/2)$$-digit numbers. This relationship can recurse
all the way down to single-digit multiplication in $$\log_2 n$$ layers where
each layer makes three calls to the layer below. In other words:

$$
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{Cost of n-digit mul}
&= 3 \cdot (\text{Cost of n/2-digit mul}) \\
&= 3 \cdot 3 \cdot (\text{Cost of n/4-digit mul}) \\
&\ldots \\
&= 3^{\log_2 n} \cdot (\text{Cost of 1-digit mul})
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
$$

Observe that:

$$
3^{\log_2 n}
= {(2^{\log_2 3})}^{\log_2 n}
= {(2^{\log_2 n})}^{\log_2 3}
= n^{\log_2 3}
$$

In other words, the asymptotic complexity of Karatsuba multiplication is
$$O(n^{\log_2 3})$$.
